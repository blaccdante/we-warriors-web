Maya's Journey

From Pain Crisis to Purpose - Becoming a Warrior Nurse

← Back to All Stories

About Maya Rodriguez

Age: 29 | Location: Houston, Texas | Profession: Registered Nurse

Sickle Cell Type: HbSS | Years as Warrior: 29 years

The Crisis That Changed Everything

It was 2 AM on a Tuesday when the familiar crushing pain began radiating through my chest and back. I was 23 years old, in my final year of nursing school, and another vaso-occlusive crisis was threatening to derail everything I had worked for. As I lay in the emergency department for what felt like the hundredth time in my life, I made a promise to myself: I would not let sickle cell disease define my limitations.

Growing up with sickle cell disease (HbSS) meant growing up with uncertainty. Some days I felt invincible, running around the playground with my friends, dreaming of becoming a superhero. Other days, I was confined to a hospital bed, watching life pass by through a sterile window. My parents, both carriers of the sickle cell trait, carried immense guilt that I never wanted them to feel. They gave me life, love, and the strength to fight – that's all any child could ask for.

Finding My Purpose in Pain

During my countless hospital stays, I was struck by the compassion of certain nurses who seemed to understand not just my physical pain, but the emotional toll of living with a chronic illness. There was Nurse Jennifer, who would sit with me during particularly difficult nights, explaining my medications and treatment plans in ways that made me feel empowered rather than powerless. There was Nurse Michael, who advocated for better pain management when doctors dismissed my 9/10 pain rating because I "looked too calm."

These angels in scrubs planted a seed that grew into an unshakeable conviction: I wanted to be that nurse for other warriors. I wanted to bridge the gap between medical knowledge and lived experience, to be an advocate and educator who understood the unique challenges we face.

The Nursing School Challenge

Nursing school with sickle cell disease was like running a marathon while carrying a backpack full of rocks. Clinical rotations were demanding – 12-hour shifts on my feet, high-stress situations, and irregular eating schedules that could trigger a crisis. I missed rotations due to hospitalizations, had to make up clinical hours, and constantly worried about whether my health would derail my dreams.

But I had a secret weapon: lived experience. While my classmates were learning about pain assessment from textbooks, I had a master's degree in understanding pain scales. When we studied blood disorders, I could share firsthand knowledge about the psychological impact of chronic illness. My professors began to see me not as a liability, but as a valuable perspective that enriched our learning environment.

Becoming the Nurse I Needed

Today, as a registered nurse specializing in hematology/oncology, I have the privilege of caring for patients with blood disorders, including many warriors with sickle cell disease. When I walk into a room and introduce myself, patients often do a double-take. "You have sickle cell too?" they ask, and I see their posture change. Suddenly, they're not just talking to a healthcare provider – they're talking to someone who gets it.

I advocate for appropriate pain management, educate families about the disease, and serve as living proof that sickle cell disease doesn't have to be a ceiling on your dreams – it can be the foundation that makes you stronger, more empathetic, and more determined.

My Message to Fellow Warriors

Your journey with sickle cell disease is not a detour from your life's path – it IS part of your path. The resilience you've developed, the perspective you've gained, and the strength you've cultivated are not side effects of your struggle; they are superpowers that make you uniquely qualified to make a difference in this world.

Yes, there will be setbacks. Yes, there will be people who doubt your capabilities. But there will also be moments when your lived experience becomes someone else's hope, when your perseverance inspires another warrior to keep fighting, and when your success opens doors for others who share this journey.

To the young warriors reading this: dream big, plan for obstacles, celebrate small victories, and never apologize for taking up space in rooms where you belong. To the parents of warriors: your child is not broken – they are equipped with a strength that will serve them well in whatever path they choose.

We are warriors, and our scars tell stories of battles won. Keep fighting, keep dreaming, and keep inspiring others with your unbreakable spirit.

Connect with Maya

Maya is currently working on initiatives to improve sickle cell education in healthcare settings and mentors young warriors pursuing healthcare careers. You can connect with her through the We Warriors community forum.
